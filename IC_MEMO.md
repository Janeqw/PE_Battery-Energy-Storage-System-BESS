## 1. Recommendation (bottom line up front)

**Conditional — pass-leaning.** We would buy a ~20% minority stake in one early-stage company. It develops ~5 megawatt (MW) distribution batteries to ready-to-build (RTB) and sells them before construction. On our independent rebuild the expected return on our shares is slightly **negative** — value-destructive at the central estimate. The cause is **price**: the proposed entry exceeds what the company is worth. Proceed to confirmatory diligence; commit only on a re-cut entry price and the [§10](#10-conditions--questions) conditions. Otherwise, pass.

| Return on our shares | Our independent view | Founders' implied claim |
|---|---|---|
| **Expected** (probability-weighted) | **−2.6% IRR · 0.88× MOIC** | ~17.7% |
| Base case | 0.0% · 1.00× | 19.4% |
| Downside (Conservative) | −15% · 0.44× | 10.7% |
| Upside (Ideal) | +4.2% · 1.23× | 23.8% |

*IRR = internal rate of return (annualised); MOIC = multiple on invested capital (proceeds ÷ investment). Figures tie to the model ([§7](#7-returns-analysis), [Appendix A](#appendix-a--methods--sources)); founder figures are claims to verify.*

**Why our number is lower.** We value the company on its **forward pipeline** — what a buyer of a development platform actually pays — not on the founders' profit-multiple. That puts the company's exit value below the price we would pay (detail in [§6](#6-valuation--deal-structure)).

**Best exit timing.** We could hold and sell later, as projects are built and operated. On a realistic, funded basis, **selling at ready-to-build (the first rung) is best for us**. Building or operating grows the company but needs new capital that dilutes our minority stake away (the value ladder, [§6](#6-valuation--deal-structure)).

**Top 3 risks**

- **Entry price** — at the proposed ~$10m post-money we overpay versus the company's ~$7.2m independent value; the 1× liquidation preference, not upside, is what protects our capital.
- **Exit / buyer** — the deep-pocketed buyers transact at 100 MW and above, not 5 MW; the founders' "interested buyers" are non-binding.
- **Concentration & founder dependence** — one early-stage company, one strategy, no diversification; execution rests almost entirely on the founder(s).

**Returns attribution.** The entire return is the development margin the company captures. None comes from merchant or operating upside — that passes to the buyer. At the central estimate our diluted stake is worth less than our cheque, so the 1× liquidation preference merely returns our cost.

**What would change the verdict**

- **A lower entry price** — a pre-money near ~$5.2m, or capital released in milestone tranches. This is the necessary lever; pipeline depth alone cannot rescue the deal ([Exhibit D](#exhibit-d--exit-value-sensitivity)).
- **Evidence on gates, buyers and the forward pipeline** — the full conditions are in [§10](#10-conditions--questions).

## 2. Investment thesis

The company captures the **development de-risking margin**: the value uplift from a bare site to an approved, grid-connected project, realised in 2–3 years per project. It sells at the RTB stage, so long-run power-price risk passes to the buyer. The niche is sub-5 MW distribution projects that large developers overlook and that benefit from an Australian Energy Market Operator (AEMO) registration exemption.

The thesis rests on two things the founders cannot prove on paper:

- **Gate clearance** — projects clear all three development gates (approval → grid connection → sale) on schedule.
- **Buyer appetite** — buyers pay the assumed prices for early-stage RTB projects, in volume.

**The reframe that decides which risks matter.** Because the company never operates the asset, merchant-price volatility is the buyer's problem, not ours. Diligence should ignore the long-run electricity-price debate. It should focus on the survival curve and the exit, plus the equity risks of backing one early-stage company.

| | Normal battery operator | **This company (develop-and-flip)** |
|---|---|---|
| What it does | Builds, then trades power ~20 years | **Develops to RTB, sells before construction** |
| Main risk | Merchant-price volatility | **Approval + exit/buyer risk** |
| Return driver | Cash yield + asset value | **Development margin, in 2–3 years** |

## 4. Industry & market

The company sells into the market for **shovel-ready development rights** for ~5 MW distribution-connected batteries (about 22 kilovolts — the everyday network, not high-voltage transmission). That market, not the electricity market, is what matters here.

### Is the market real and growing? Yes.

Coal retirement plus the solar and wind build-out create a structural need for storage, backed by firm government targets. For a developer that builds and sells, fast growth is a tailwind — more buyers. The 5 MW niche is a distinct, smaller slice of total storage demand.

| Storage segment (AEMO ISP 2026, Step Change) | 2026 | 2030 | 2050 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Small-scale / distributed (home + community) | 5 GW | 12 GW | 35 GW |
| Grid-scale (~100 MW+) | ~45 GW in the connection queue | — | ~40 GW of need |

The capital and the deals sit overwhelmingly at the grid-scale end — which leads to the buyer question below. *(Source: AEMO Integrated System Plan 2026 via trade press; [Appendix A](#appendix-a--methods--sources).)*

### Is the niche defensible? Plausibly — verify.

Large developers chase 100 MW+ transmission projects, leaving ~5 MW distribution under-contested. Sub-5 MW projects are exempt from a layer of AEMO registration, which should speed approval. This edge needs checking: confirm the white space, the site access, and the network relationships the founders claim.

### The decisive risk: will there be buyers?

The buyer pool for Australian battery assets is real and well-funded — independent power producers, infrastructure funds, super funds, government green-capital bodies and retailers. Two cautions dominate.

- **Bindingness** — the founders' "3 interested buyers" are not contractually committed.
- **Scale and stage mismatch** — the deep-pocketed buyers transact at scale, and increasingly pay up for *contracted, construction-ready* assets, not raw RTB.

The recent deal record shows the scale gap plainly:

| Recent deal / project | Size | Buyer |
|---|---|---|
| Summerfield (SA) | 240 MW | Palisade, with CEFC / Aware Super / HESTA |
| Supernode Stage 1 (Qld) | 260 MW | Origin (12-year tolling contract) |
| Energy Security Corporation platform (NSW) | 200 MW | Energy Security Corporation |
| Ebor (NSW) | 100 MW | NSW long-term energy tender |
| A community / distribution battery | ~5 MW | Network company / government programme / aggregator |

![Recent battery deals are large](outputs/figures/buyer_sizes.png)

A single 5 MW asset is too small for an infrastructure or super fund: the diligence effort is much the same as for a 200 MW asset, so they favour size. The natural buyers of individual 5 MW batteries are a thinner, often grant-dependent pool. **The mitigation — and the catch:** the company can bundle its ~35 projects (~175 MW) into one portfolio to reach those buyers. That turns the exit into a single, all-or-nothing platform sale — which is exactly what the forward-pipeline valuation in [§6](#6-valuation--deal-structure) prices. *(Source: energy trade press; [Appendix A](#appendix-a--methods--sources).)*

### Can they execute? The gate question.

A flip must clear three separate gates, and true success is their product. The founders' headline **40 / 65 / 80%** is the development-approval (DA) gate **only** — not whole-funnel success.

| Gate (Base case) | Pass rate |
|---|---|
| Development approval (the founders' headline) | 65% |
| Grid connection | 70% |
| Reach a sale | 80% |
| **True whole-funnel flip success** | **36.4%** |
| *Independent benchmark (approval at 80%)* | *44.8%* |

![Survival curve — independent vs the founders' base case](outputs/figures/survival_curve.png)

So the 65% headline overstates whole-funnel success. The right response is not to dismiss the company, but to ask for project-by-project, per-state evidence on each gate. *(Method: probability-of-default / survival analysis, credit-risk practice; [Appendix A](#appendix-a--methods--sources).)*

## 5. Business model & value-chain choice

The same ~5 MW asset can be played at different points on the value chain — which are the **rungs of the value ladder** ([§6](#6-valuation--deal-structure)): develop-and-flip is selling at R1, build-and-sell at R2, own-and-operate at R3. The table below shows the risk-adjusted **business** return at each, which frames where on the ladder it makes sense to exit.

| Business model | Hold | Expected IRR | Expected MOIC | Downside IRR | Character |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Develop & flip** (this company) | ~3 yrs | 3.7% | 1.12× | −10.6% | Weakest risk-adjusted; approvals + a buyer must land |
| Build & sell | ~1.5 yrs | 25.9% | 1.41× | −18.2% | Highest expected, most fragile; thin build margin |
| Own & operate (contracted) | ~15 yrs | 8.1% | 2.41× | +4.4% | Only standalone model positive in its downside |
| Integrated (develop→build→operate) | ~18 yrs | 12.2% | 4.41× | +8.3% | All risks stacked, but no exit risk; longest lock-up |

![Risk-return by business model](outputs/figures/stage_comparison.png)

*These are risk-adjusted **business** returns on the asset, not our diluted shareholder return. Full workings: [`STAGE_COMPARISON.md`](financial_models/STAGE_COMPARISON.md).*

**The read.** Develop-and-flip is the **weakest** risk-adjusted model of the four, and the only gated one (the others buy past development at market price). For patient private capital, own-and-operate (contracted) is the natural core — the only standalone model that stays positive in its downside, and it plays to a credit-risk edge (judging the offtake counterparty is serviceability analysis). Backing this company is justified only on the [§10](#10-conditions--questions) conditions, with downside terms doing real work. **Watch-point:** if the founders keep the best projects to operate and flip the rest, ask how "best" is chosen — so the flip pool is not left the weaker assets.

## 6. Valuation & deal structure

The valuation runs in two steps. First, value the whole company on the primary **forward-pipeline** basis. Then apply the cap table to get the value of our shares.

**Why the forward pipeline, not a profit multiple.** A develop-and-flip company is a development platform. A buyer pays for its **forward pipeline** of future projects — the risk-adjusted net present value (rNPV) of the projects still in flight — plus retained cash, less debt. It does not pay a multiple of profit already earned. An earlier draft valued the company on a multiple of past profit; that overstated value and rested on one unsourced number. The forward-pipeline basis is what values the company if we sell at the first rung of the ladder below.

### The value ladder — when do we sell our shares?

We own the company as its projects climb the lifecycle, and we can sell at any rung. Each later rung removes more risk but costs more time and capital. Two views: **Option A** holds our ownership % constant (it ignores the capital needed to climb); **Option B** funds the climb, so new equity dilutes us and debt (borrowing that ranks ahead of equity) sits in front of us.

| Rung — we sell after… | Valuation method | Company equity | Our share (A) | MOIC (A) | IRR (A) | Years |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **R1 — approval** (ready-to-build) | forward-pipeline rNPV | ~$7m | ~$1.8m | 0.88× | −5% | ~2.5 |
| **R2 — construction** (built asset) | built-asset value − all-in cost | ~$28m | ~$4.3m | 2.13× | +21% | ~4 |
| **R3 — operating** (stabilised) | income basis (cash flows / yield) | ~$23m | ~$3.7m | 1.84× | +9% | ~7 |

*First-Chicago weighted. R1 reuses the forward-pipeline build (detail below) and ties to the [§7](#7-returns-analysis) headline (0.88× MOIC). R2/R3 reuse the stage engine ([STAGE_COMPARISON.md](financial_models/STAGE_COMPARISON.md)) and are illustrative uplifts. Best rung under Option A: **R2 (build & sell)**.*

**Option B — the funding drag.** Building and operating need large new capital. The company raises equity (diluting our ~14.4% stake) and debt (ahead of us), so our slice shrinks even as the pie grows:

| Rung | Our % (A) | Our % (B) | MOIC (A) | MOIC (B) | Funding drag |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| R1 (RTB) | 14.4% | 14.4% | 0.88× | 0.88× | none |
| R2 (built) | 14.4% | ~4.3% | 2.13× | 0.60× | −1.53× |
| R3 (operating) | 14.4% | ~2.2% | 1.84× | 0.25× | −1.59× |

Holding longer grows the **pie** (company equity ~$7m → ~$28m) but shrinks **our slice** (dilution to ~2%). After funding, the best rung flips to **R1 — sell at ready-to-build**: the only rung where a minority holder is not diluted away. The Option-B dilution and the stabilised-asset premium are `[[TO CONFIRM]]`.

### R1 in detail — the forward-pipeline exit equity (Base case)

| Build-up | Value |
|---|---|
| Forward-pipeline rNPV at exit (~25 projects in flight × ~$0.10m each) | ~$2.4m |
| + Net cash retained at exit (realised profit not distributed) | ~$4.8m |
| − Debt at exit | ~$0.0m |
| **Company exit equity (Base)** | **~$7.2m** |

*Same gates and discount rate as the rest of the model. Pipeline depth at exit is `[[TO CONFIRM]]` and is the dominant assumption ([Exhibit D](#exhibit-d--exit-value-sensitivity)). Conservative case: realised profit turns negative, so exit equity is only the residual forward pipeline (~$0.9m).*

### Step 2 — the cap table → our proceeds (Base case)

| Bridge to our proceeds | Value |
|---|---|
| Company exit equity | ~$7.2m |
| × our diluted ownership (14.4%) = as-converted share | ~$1.04m |
| 1× liquidation preference floor (our $2m) | $2.00m |
| **Our proceeds (greater of the two)** | **~$2.00m** |

Our as-converted stake is worth less than our cheque, so the preference binds: at Base we get our money back, no more. Full line-by-line bridge: [Exhibit 3](#exhibit-3--return-bridge-base-case).

### Are we overpaying? The entry-price check

The result is underwater because of day-one price. The check only means something if the two numbers come from **different sources** — the proposed price is the ask; the independent value is our own estimate.

| | Amount | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Proposed price (post-money) | ~$10.0m | Placeholder (reframe; no founder price exists) |
| Independent value (forward-pipeline) | ~$7.2m | Our model — but price inputs unverified (see below) |
| **Entry-price gap (overpayment)** | **~$2.8m** | proposed − independent |

The lever is price. Company value equals post-money at a pre-money near **~$5.2m**; our diluted stake only beats our cheque below a pre-money of **~$3.2m**. Re-price toward this, or release capital in milestone tranches.

### Cross-checks

| Cross-check | Result | Note |
|---|---|---|
| Earnings multiple (run-rate ~$1.6m/yr × 3 / 5 / 7×, + retained − debt) | ~$9.7m / $12.9m / $16.1m | More generous than the pipeline basis; we anchor on the conservative pipeline figure |
| Per-pipeline asset value (rNPV · $/MW · VC method) | $0.45m – $0.58m | Sanity-checks the development premium on one representative pipeline |
| Comparable transactions | not available | No credible developer-platform comps (paid databases out of scope); pipeline basis stands |

### Provenance — the "independent" value is not yet fully independent

Two price inputs to the rNPV — the RTB sale price and the development cost — are the founders' **unverified claims** (tagged *Proposed (manager)*). Until each is verified against a primary source and re-tagged *Independent (verified)*, the ~$7.2m is illustrative. The model's contamination guard refuses to treat it as verified. Because the founders' prices are optimistic, verifying them is **more likely to lower the value than raise it** — so the overpayment gap is, if anything, understated. Key assumptions and their provenance: [Appendix C, Key assumptions](#key-assumptions).

## 7. Returns analysis

Returns are modelled bottom-up: survival curve → company exit equity (forward-pipeline basis) → cap table with dilution and the 1× liquidation preference → return on our shares. Scenarios are probability-weighted by the First-Chicago method. The figures below are the **R1 (sell at ready-to-build)** return; returns for selling later (R2 built, R3 operating), under both funding options, are in the value ladder ([§6](#6-valuation--deal-structure)).

| Scenario | Flip success | Company exit equity | Our proceeds | MOIC | IRR |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Conservative | 22.4% | $0.9m | $0.89m | 0.44× | −15% |
| Base | 36.4% | $7.2m | $2.00m | 1.00× | 0.0% |
| Ideal | 44.8% | $17.0m | $2.45m | 1.23× | +4.2% |
| **First-Chicago expected** (30 / 50 / 20) | — | — | — | **0.88×** | **−2.6%** |

![Equity IRR on our shares, by scenario](outputs/figures/irr_by_scenario.png)

**Attribution.** The value is entirely the development margin the company captures; nothing relies on merchant or operating upside. At Base the company is worth less than the ~$10m we pay, so our diluted stake is under water and the 1× preference returns ~$2.0m — our cost. The downside is a deep loss; a total loss if even the residual forward pipeline finds no buyer. Line-by-line: [Exhibit 3](#exhibit-3--return-bridge-base-case).

**This is conditional analysis.** The headline rests on three `[[TO CONFIRM]]` inputs — pre-money, pipeline depth at exit, and the distribution policy. Each materially moves the result ([Exhibit D](#exhibit-d--exit-value-sensitivity)). It is a finding about the current terms, not a verdict on the company.

**The preference both protects and caps us.** Across Base and Ideal it binds or nearly binds, flooring us near break-even; our upside opens only once the company is worth well above what we pay.

**Distribution-policy risk.** The model assumes no interim distributions (the conservative read). Pro-rata distributions would *help* us, since they add on top of the preference floor. The real risk runs the other way: a founder-controlled startup may pay little or no pro-rata cash, leaving us reliant on the preference. Confirm pro-rata sharing ([§10](#10-conditions--questions)).

## 8. Risks & mitigants

| Risk | Severity | Mitigant required |
|---|---|---|
| **Entry price** | High | Re-cut the pre-money toward independent value, or milestone-tranche capital |
| **Exit / buyer** | High | ≥3–4 contractually-progressing buyers; independent price comps; a credible portfolio-aggregation exit |
| **Single-company concentration** | High | Size as a small, risk-tolerant allocation; not a diversified holding |
| **Founder & key-person** | High | Key-person provisions, founder vesting, information rights |
| **Development / approval (gates)** | High | Per-state, sub-5 MW approval and connection track record; stress a 6–12-month slip |
| **Unverified prices** | High | Verify RTB price and dev cost against primary sources before relying on the value |
| **Down-round / dilution** | Medium–High | Pre-emption and anti-dilution rights; agreed option-pool size |
| **Minority rights / governance** | Medium–High | Board seat or observer, information rights, veto over major decisions |
| **Illiquidity** | Medium–High | Accept only for a risk-tolerant allocation; seek tag-along / drag-along |
| **Policy dependency** | Medium–High | Diversify across three states; treat the sub-5 MW exemption as verify-and-monitor |

*Not the main risk:* merchant-price volatility — it passes to the buyer.

**Stress case.** At Base our return is break-even (the preference returns our cost). In the Conservative case realised profit turns negative, the company is worth only its residual forward pipeline, and we suffer a deep loss — a total loss if that pipeline finds no buyer. A thinner pipeline at exit, lower RTB prices, or a higher discount rate push the expected return further below water ([Exhibit D](#exhibit-d--exit-value-sensitivity)). Survivable only because the position is sized as a small, risk-tolerant allocation.

**Downside protections to secure:** a 1× non-participating liquidation preference; anti-dilution; an agreed option-pool size. Because this is one startup, not a fund, weight the failure scenario more heavily and demand stronger terms than a diversified commitment would need.

## 9. Exit — how the money comes back

We hold shares, so our money returns only when the company sells — and *when* it sells (which ladder rung) sets who the buyer is and what we get ([§6](#6-valuation--deal-structure)):

| Exit rung | What is sold | Likely buyer |
|---|---|---|
| R1 — ready-to-build | Approved development rights | Developers / funds buying RTB pipelines |
| R2 — built | A finished, barely-operated battery | Infrastructure funds, operators |
| R3 — operating | A cash-flowing, stabilised asset | Infrastructure / superannuation funds buying yield |

- **Trade sale of the company** — the primary route, most likely after the ~175 MW pipeline is aggregated into a saleable platform; we are paid alongside other shareholders.
- **Secondary sale of our shares** — selling our minority stake earlier; uncertain, and likely at a discount.

The **liquidation preference governs the split**: on a sale we take the greater of our $2m back or our diluted share of exit equity, capped by what is available. At Base the preference returns ~$2.0m; in the Conservative case it recovers only ~$0.9m. Our exit depends on the company's own exit, which depends on the buyer pool ([§4](#4-industry--market)). Treat a demonstrated buyer path and clear transfer / drag-along / tag-along rights as gating conditions.

## 10. Conditions & questions

**Recommendation: Conditional — pass-leaning.** Commit only as a small, risk-tolerant allocation, and only if every condition is met. Absent strong evidence — above all a re-cut entry price — the default is **Pass**.

**Commit only if (any one unmet ⇒ Pass):**

1. **Re-priced entry** — a lower pre-money (toward ~$5.2m) or milestone-tranched capital, so we do not pay ~$10m for a ~$7.2m company. This is the primary lever; pipeline depth alone does not rescue the deal.
2. **Equity terms** — pre-money, share class (preferred), price per share, a 1× non-participating liquidation preference, option-pool size, and anti-dilution / pre-emption.
3. **Governance** — a board seat or observer, information rights, and veto over major decisions.
4. **Exit depth & pricing** — ≥3–4 contractually-progressing RTB buyers and independent price comps; a credible portfolio-aggregation exit.
5. **Gate evidence** — per-state, sub-5 MW evidence for the approval, connection and sale gates.
6. **Survivable downside** — the Conservative case and a stressed Base impair no more than the allocated amount.

**Approval sought:** authority to conduct confirmatory diligence and, if every condition is met, to invest up to ~$2m `[[TO CONFIRM]]` for a ~20% stake as a small, risk-tolerant allocation.

**Questions for the founders**

- The **pre-money** and how it was derived; the **price per share** and our resulting ownership.
- The **share class** and whether it carries a 1× non-participating liquidation preference.
- **Option-pool size** and expected future-round dilution; is anti-dilution / pre-emption available?
- **Governance** — board seat, information rights, veto provisions; founder vesting and key-person terms.
- Per-state, sub-5 MW **approval, connection and sale** track record.
- Of the "3 interested buyers", what is **contractual** (signed options, price, terms)?
- **Independent comps** behind the RTB prices, and the bottom-up **development-cost** build-up.
- The **distribution policy** — do we share **pro-rata** in interim cash, or is profit reinvested / skewed to management?
- How deep is the **forward pipeline at exit** (the dominant value driver)?
- The realistic **exit route and timing**, and our transfer / drag-along / tag-along rights.

## Appendix A — Methods & sources

**What this analysis is built on.** A reproducible Python pipeline over free public Australian data, feeding a formula-driven Excel model. The Python engine reproduces the workbook cell-for-cell; `make all` regenerates the data, model and figures.

**Methods (recognised techniques, not bespoke).**

| Technique | Used for |
|---|---|
| Probability-of-default / survival curve (Basel, IFRS 9) | Whole-funnel flip success = approval × connection × sale |
| Risk-adjusted net present value (rNPV) | Per-project and forward-pipeline value |
| Forward-pipeline exit basis | Company exit equity = forward rNPV + retained cash − debt |
| Pre-/post-money, cap table, dilution, liquidation preference | Our ownership and proceeds |
| First-Chicago scenario weighting | Probability-weighted expected return |
| Build-up discount rate (CFA) | 18.8% = RBA 10-year CGS yield 4.8% + 14.0% development risk premium |

## Appendix B — Glossary

- **IRR** — internal rate of return; the annualised return on our shares.
- **MOIC** — multiple on invested capital; proceeds ÷ investment.
- **RTB (ready-to-build)** — a project taken through approval and grid connection, sold before construction.
- **DA gate** — development-approval gate; the founders' 40 / 65 / 80% measure this gate only.
- **Whole-funnel flip success** — the product of the three gates (approval, grid connection, sale); because they multiply, true success is far below the single-gate 65% headline (see [§4](#4-industry--market)).
- **Forward-pipeline rNPV** — risk-adjusted value of projects still in flight at exit; the primary exit basis.
- **Pre-money / post-money** — the company's value before / after our investment goes in.
- **Dilution** — the fall in our ownership when new shares are issued (option pool, future round); 20.0% → 14.4%.
- **Liquidation preference (1× non-participating)** — on a sale we take the greater of our money back or our as-converted share.
- **First-Chicago method** — probability-weighting scenario outcomes (30 / 50 / 20) into one expected figure.
- **Value ladder / R1 · R2 · R3** — the staged exit choice: sell after approval (R1, ready-to-build), after construction (R2, built), or after operating (R3, stabilised). Option A holds our % constant; Option B funds the climb (new equity dilutes us, debt ranks ahead).
